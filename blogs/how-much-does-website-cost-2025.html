04 Technical Limitations Create Invisible Ceilings

Site builders impose technical SEO restrictions that limit growth potential. Here's what this means for your business: SEO (Search Engine Optimization) is how Google finds and ranks your website. When potential customers search for your services, you want to appear at the top of results. However, Squarespace only allows you to write custom descriptions for your main pages—not individual service pages or blog posts—which violates Google's best practices[10]. This means if you offer multiple services, Google won't properly understand what each page is about. Additionally, both Wix and Squarespace automatically create your site's "roadmap" (XML sitemap) that tells Google which pages to look at, but they don't let you customize it. With a custom website, we have complete control over these technical details, ensuring every page is optimized to attract the right customers searching for exactly what you offer.

06 Hidden Costs Compound When Businesses Scale

Perhaps most revealing, 51% of website projects are abandoned before completion, with 91% of users finding some aspect of website building overly difficult[19]. This high abandonment rate foreshadows a larger pattern: businesses typically outgrow site builders within 12-24 months as they scale operations.

When migration becomes necessary, the costs prove substantial. Here's why: When you outgrow a site builder (which typically happens within 18-24 months), you can't simply "move" your website—you have to completely rebuild it from scratch. Site builders use proprietary code that can't be exported, meaning all your content, design, and functionality must be recreated. Rebuilding costs run 25-40% higher than initial custom development[20] because developers must first analyze your existing site, recreate all functionality, manually transfer content, and often work around limitations created by the original platform. This includes redesign, content migration, SEO preservation, and functionality recreation—all labor-intensive tasks that could have been avoided. Essentially, you're paying twice: once for the site builder that no longer meets your needs, and again for the professional website you should have invested in originally.
